Case-Based Question 1: Zitkala-Ša's long hair was cut against her will at the boarding school.

Question: What does this incident reveal about the treatment of Native Americans?
Answer: The incident shows how Native Americans were forced to abandon their cultural identity. It highlights racial oppression and lack of respect for indigenous traditions.
Case-Based Question 2: Bama notices an elder carrying food with a string without touching it.

Question: What social practice does this reflect?
Answer: It reflects caste-based discrimination where Dalits were considered 'polluting' and treated as inferior, revealing deep-rooted social injustice.

Short Answer Questions 5 Questions

Jump to:
1. Why did Zitkala-Ša resist the cutting of her hair?
Answer: Zitkala-Ša resisted because long hair symbolized dignity and identity in her culture. Cutting it meant humiliation and loss of self-respect.
2. How did the boarding school suppress native culture?
Answer: The boarding school imposed Western customs, forced haircutting, and strict discipline to erase native identity and culture.
3. What incident made Bama aware of caste discrimination?
Answer: Bama saw an upper-caste man carrying food with a string to avoid touching it, which revealed caste-based untouchability.
4. How did Bama's brother guide her?
Answer: He advised Bama to study hard and rise above discrimination through education and self-respect.
5. What common theme unites both narratives?
Answer: Both narratives show oppression of marginalized communities and stress the importance of resistance and dignity.

Long Answer Questions 3 Questions

1. Discuss the theme of identity and oppression in Memories of Childhood.
Answer: The chapter presents two personal accounts of childhood trauma caused by social injustice. Zitkala-Ša experiences racial oppression when her hair is cut, symbolizing loss of identity. Bama faces caste discrimination in everyday life. Both narratives reveal how dominant cultures suppress marginalized communities. However, they also emphasize resistance, awareness, and the power of education to reclaim dignity and identity.
2. Compare the experiences of Zitkala-Ša and Bama.
Answer: Zitkala-Ša suffers racial discrimination in a colonial boarding school, while Bama faces caste-based oppression in Indian society. Though contexts differ, both experience humiliation and injustice. Their stories highlight similar struggles against social dominance and emphasize courage and self-realization.
3. How does education become a tool of empowerment in the chapter?
Answer: Education plays a vital role in empowering the oppressed. Bama's brother advises her to study hard to overcome discrimination. Awareness and learning help both writers understand injustice and fight it with dignity.
