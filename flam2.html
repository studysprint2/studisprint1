PART B: Case-Based Questions (2)

Case-Based Question 1: Saheb searches for valuables in garbage every morning. Once, the author jokingly promised to open a school for him. Later, when Saheb asked if the school was ready, the author felt ashamed.

Why did the author feel ashamed and what does this reveal about society?

Answer: The author felt ashamed because her promise was casual and not meant seriously, but Saheb took it as real hope. This shows how poor children cling to small promises for a better future, while society often fails to fulfil its responsibilities toward them.

Case-Based Question 2: Mukesh, a boy from a bangle-making family in Firozabad, dreams of becoming a motor mechanic and is willing to walk long distances to learn.

What does Mukesh's dream reveal about his character?

Answer: Mukesh is ambitious, determined, and courageous. Unlike others who accept poverty as fate, he wants to break free from his family's traditional occupation. His dream reflects hope, self-belief, and a desire for a better life.

PART D: Short Answer Questions (5)

Why does Saheb scrounge through garbage?

Answer: Saheb scrounges through garbage to survive. The garbage provides him food, money, and useful items. For him, garbage is a source of livelihood and hope, not waste. Poverty forces him into this work at a very young age.

Why did Saheb's family migrate to India?

Answer: Saheb's family migrated from Bangladesh after storms destroyed their fields and livelihood. With no means of survival left, they moved to India hoping for a better life but ended up living in slums and working as ragpickers.

Why are children in Seemapuri barefoot?

Answer: Children in Seemapuri remain barefoot due to extreme poverty. Though they may own footwear, they rarely use it. Being barefoot becomes a symbol of their deprivation rather than a cultural habit.

Describe the working conditions of bangle makers.

Answer: Bangle makers work in dark, poorly ventilated rooms near hot furnaces. The working conditions are unhealthy and dangerous. Many children lose their eyesight early and remain trapped in poverty due to exploitation and lack of education.

What is the significance of the title Lost Spring?

Answer: The title symbolises the lost childhood of poor children who are forced to work for survival. Instead of enjoying education and play, they face hardship and responsibility at a very young age.

PART E: Long Answer Questions (3)

Describe the life of ragpickers in Seemapuri.

Answer: Seemapuri is a slum on the outskirts of Delhi where thousands of ragpickers live in extreme poverty. They survive by collecting waste from garbage dumps. Their homes are made of tin, mud, and plastic sheets, without basic facilities like clean water or sanitation. Children grow up working instead of studying. Garbage becomes their main source of livelihood and hope. Although they have ration cards, they lack identity and security. The author highlights how poverty steals their childhood and forces them into a life of struggle and deprivation.

How does Mukesh represent hope in the story?

Answer: Mukesh, unlike other children in Firozabad, dares to dream of a better future. Born into a family of bangle makers, he refuses to accept the dangerous and exploitative profession. He wants to become a motor mechanic and is willing to walk long distances to learn the skill. His determination shows courage and a desire to break free from the cycle of poverty. Mukesh represents hope, ambition, and the possibility of change even in harsh circumstances.

Discuss the main theme of Lost Spring.

Answer: Lost Spring highlights the exploitation of poor children and the loss of their childhood due to poverty. Anees Jung presents the lives of Saheb and Mukesh to show how children are forced into labour instead of education. Saheb scavenges garbage while Mukesh works in bangle factories. Both are victims of social injustice and economic inequality. The story exposes how society fails to protect children's rights and dreams. The title symbolises the loss of innocence, joy, and opportunities that should define childhood.
